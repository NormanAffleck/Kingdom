The Gravity Model of Trade

A physics-inspired equation that predicts how much two countries will trade — using only their size and the distance between them.

Project Overview

What is this about?

Inspired by Cornell Notes on introductory international economics, this project demonstrates the Gravity Model of Trade — one of the most robust empirical findings in economics.

The model borrows from Newton's law of gravitation: two bodies attract in proportion to their masses and inversely to distance. In trade: two countries exchange goods in proportion to their GDP and inversely to the geographic distance between them.

What makes this remarkable: despite all our technology, trade volume is still heavily shaped by where you are on the map.

The Gravity Equation
Tradeij = G × (GDPi × GDPj) / Distanceij
G = gravitational constant GDPi, GDPj = economic sizes Distance = km between capitals

📝 From Cornell Notes

Large economies import more due to high incomes and attract more because they produce diverse goods. Distance increases transport costs and reduces personal contact.

🌐 The Distance Paradox

Despite the internet, a 1% increase in distance is still associated with a 0.7–1% fall in trade. Technology has softened geography — but not eliminated it.

🏴 Border Effects

Canadian provinces trade far more with each other than nearby US states at the same distance. A border acts like 1,500–2,500 extra km.

📱 iPhone: GVC in Action

Design in USA → components from Korea/Taiwan → assembly in China → sold worldwide. Complex trade flows the basic gravity model must adapt for.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

Physics meets economics

GDP drives purchasing power and variety; distance drives costs and friction. Together they explain most global trade flows.

Skills

Empirical modeling

I learned to read and apply a model — not just memorize it. The calculator let me test whether intuitions about trade partners match the formula.

Reflection

Surprised by geography

I assumed globalization meant distance didn't matter. The gravity model proved me wrong. Even today, where you are shapes who you trade with more than any other factor.

🍕 The Pizza Optimizer

A binary integer programming problem: choose pizza toppings to maximize satisfaction within a 7ww budget. The classic "knapsack problem" in disguise.

Why did we solve this problem first?

The pizza optimizer is a hands-on introduction to mathematical modeling. Before you can optimize a supply chain or allocate a hospital's resources, you need to understand the structure: a set of choices, parameters describing each choice, decision variables, an objective function to maximize, and a constraint that limits you. Pizza makes these abstract concepts concrete and tangible.

Part 5 — Model Components with Notation

Using Chapter 4 notation, here is the formal definition of the pizza optimization model:

𝒯 = {t₁, t₂, ..., t₁₀}
Set
The collection of all 10 available pizza toppings from which we may select.
cᵢ, sᵢ
Parameters
Known values: cᵢ = cost of topping i (in ww) · sᵢ = satisfaction score of topping i
xᵢ ∈ {0, 1}
Decision Variables
Binary choice: xᵢ = 1 if topping i is selected, xᵢ = 0 if not selected.
B = 7.00 ww
Constraint
Total cost of selected toppings must not exceed the budget B: Σ(cᵢ · xᵢ) ≤ B
// Objective Function — Maximize total satisfaction
max Z = Σ sᵢ · xᵢ   for all i ∈ 𝒯

// Subject to budget constraint
Σ cᵢ · xᵢ ≤ 7.00   (budget in ww)

// Binary requirement
xᵢ ∈ {0, 1}   for all i ∈ 𝒯

Sensitivity Analysis

Price Creep — At what price does Salame stop being worth it?

Salame has the highest satisfaction score (8.3) at 2.0ww. Testing price increments of 0.25ww: at 2.25ww Salame still enters the solution. At 2.50ww, the budget gets tight and Zucchini must be dropped to compensate — satisfaction falls. At 3.00ww, Parma ham (7.2 score) at the same price becomes a serious competitor. Salame stays worth it until roughly 3.50ww, where the opportunity cost of including it causes a net satisfaction loss versus alternative combinations.

Satisfaction Score Tipping Points

Fried eggplant (in optimal, score = 5.2 at 2.0ww): Its satisfaction/cost ratio is 2.6. It stays in the solution until its score drops below ~4.0, at which point Pancetta (4.2 at same cost) would replace it.

Burrata (not in optimal, score = 4.7 at 3.0ww): Its ratio is only 1.57 — too low. For Burrata to enter, its score would need to rise above ~6.0, giving it a ratio competitive with current selections.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

Binary LP structure

The pizza problem is a classic 0-1 knapsack problem. The structure — binary variables, single budget constraint, maximize sum of values — appears in portfolio selection, project selection, and facility location problems.

Skills

Why greedy fails

Sorting by satisfaction/cost ratio (greedy) and picking top items can fail. Parma ham has a ratio of 2.4 but at 3.0ww it crowds out two cheaper items with a combined score of 8.0+. Greedy isn't optimal for binary problems.

Reflection

Models vs. intuition

I wouldn't have picked Zucchini (score 3.5) intuitively — it seems mediocre. But at 1.0ww it fills budget gaps efficiently. The model caught what intuition missed. That's the whole point of quantitative analysis.
